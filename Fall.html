Book Chapter: Seeing God Clearly

The Doctrine of Progression: Why the Fall Was a Forward Step

What if the story you've been told about the beginning‚Äîabout God, about Adam and Eve, about good and evil‚Äîwas not a tragedy, but the essential first step in a magnificent plan of progression? What if confusion in religion stems not from God, but from losing the simple, revelatory key to understanding Him?

1. The Art of Unprepared Speaking

You don't build a talk in an hour. You build it over a lifetime. I once heard a story about a teacher asked to give a three-hour lecture. When praised for his flawless delivery, he was asked how long it took to prepare. "Five minutes," he said. After the shocked silence, he clarified: "That talk took me thirty years to live and learn. The last five minutes were just to arrange it."

That's how real wisdom works. It's the iceberg beneath the surface. When I speak, whether from a pulpit or into a microphone, I'm not reciting a script. I'm drawing from that deep well of accrued experience and revelation, organizing ideas, not memorizing words. The preparation happens in the living, not just the thinking.

2. The Simple, Startling Image of God

Let's start with the most logical, yet most contested, point of all: What is God like? The world is full of complicated creeds describing an unknowable, formless being. But the simplest scripture cuts through the noise: "Man was created in the image of God."

Think about that. If you look into a pond, you see your image‚Äîyour form, your face. If we are created in God's image, then the form we see reflected is, in essence, His form.

This isn't philosophical mystery. The Bible shows it repeatedly: God spoke to Moses "face to face, as a man speaketh unto his friend." You need a face for that. Stephen, at his death, looked up and saw God, with Jesus standing on His "right hand." You need a form for that. A child understands this immediately.

If you look upon God, you would see a man... We were created in the image and likeness of God. That's the big secret. But it's not a secret at all.

3. The Source of Confusion: Interpretation Without Revelation

This leads us to the great conundrum. If the Bible is our source, but its interpretations are wildly different, who is right? Methodists, Baptists, Presbyterians‚Äîall sincere, all using the same book to reach different conclusions. If God is not the author of confusion, then the problem must be in the interpretation.

How was scripture originally given? By revelation from God to prophets. How was it written? By inspiration. It was then translated by men across languages and centuries‚Äîa noble but human process, prone to deficiencies.

So, how do we get back to the original, correct meaning? The same way it was received: by revelation. You cannot correctly interpret divine communication without the divine Communicator. Personal revelation isn't a bonus; it's the essential mechanism for discerning truth from error.

Personal Stories & Experiences

The Thirty-Year Talk

I heard about a man named Vaughan Featherstone. He was asked to give a three-hour seminar. He delivered it flawlessly‚Äîsuccinct, scriptural, powerful. Afterwards, someone asked him how long it took to prepare. He said, "About five minutes." The person was shocked. How could you create that in five minutes? Vaughan corrected himself. He said, "That talk actually took me 20 or 30 years to develop. The last five minutes was just to put together the content in a succinct sequence so that I could share it." When I heard that, it reinforced a huge idea for me. What you share in any given moment is just the tip of the iceberg. The real substance is the vast amount of knowledge and experience you've accrued along the way.

The Point

This shows that authentic wisdom and teaching cannot be rushed or faked. It's built through lived experience over time.

Lesson Learned

Don't worry about having a perfectly prepared script. Focus on filling your life with real learning, experience, and revelation. Your "preparation" is your life. When you speak from that deep well, the organization of it becomes simple.

My Isaiah Revelation

Someone once gave me a book claiming to explain Isaiah. I read it and was disappointed‚Äîit felt empty, like "doctrines of men." Later, I was reading a specific chapter of Isaiah with a different, divinely-guided interpretation in mind. As I read verse one, I started feeling the Spirit. Verse two, three, it continued. It was palpable, thick, and rich. This feeling lasted the entire chapter, all the way to the last verse. When I said "Amen," the Spirit lingered and then faded. That was my revelation. It wasn't an idea; it was an experience. It sealed the truth of that interpretation in my heart more powerfully than any argument ever could.

The Point

This story demonstrates the tangible, experiential difference between intellectual study and receiving a spiritual witness through personal revelation.
