Step 9: The Courage to Make Things Right

A Practical Guide to Healing Relationships Through Direct Restitution

We often carry the weight of past mistakes like invisible backpacks filled with stones. Step 9 isn't about continuing to carry that weight; it's about learning how to put those stones down, one by one.

The Foundation of Making Amends

Technical perfection isn't the goal in recovery—I learned this firsthand when dealing with microphone issues during our sessions. We use OBS Studio for higher quality recordings because Zoom's default quality simply doesn't capture the nuance needed for this work. But here's what matters more than perfect audio: showing up authentically and doing the work.

Step 9 requires making direct restitution to all persons we have harmed, wherever possible. This isn't a theoretical exercise; it's practical, boots-on-the-ground relationship repair. Like the repentant sons of Mosiah in scripture, we must seek forgiveness with genuine humility.

Key Principle: Making amends should never be impulsive or careless. This isn't about clearing your conscience at someone else's expense. It's about prayerfully considering when and how to approach each person, often consulting with sponsors or trusted advisors who can provide perspective you might lack in your eagerness to make things right.

Navigating Practical Realities

  • When Direct Contact Is Possible: Face-to-face meetings are recommended whenever feasible. There's something powerful about looking someone in the eyes, without the buffer of technology, and saying, "I was wrong, and I'm sorry." This approach respects the gravity of what you're attempting to repair.
  • When Direct Contact Isn't Possible: Legal restrictions may prevent meeting with some people. Others may have moved, passed away, or made it clear they don't want contact. In these cases, we explore indirect amends—writing letters we never send, helping someone who reminds us of that person, or making charitable donations in their name.
  • The Exception Rule: Certain serious matters—theft, abuse, or actions with potential legal repercussions—require ecclesiastical or professional counsel before taking action. Your sponsor isn't a substitute for legal advice when actual crimes are involved.

    • The Living Amend

    Perhaps the most powerful form of restitution is what I call "the living amend." This involves adopting recovery principles into your daily life so thoroughly that everyone around you is blessed by your transformation. It's the amend you make not through a single conversation, but through who you become.

    I once made an insensitive comment to a widow about dating—a thoughtless remark that damaged our relationship for two years despite my immediate apology. The words hung between us like a wall. Sometimes, even sincere apologies don't immediately heal what's been broken. People choose whether to be offended, and our job is to focus on our own actions rather than their reactions.

    Personal Stories

    Real-life examples and lessons learned

    Story 1: The Two-Year Wall

    The Story: I described an incident where I made an insensitive comment to a widow about dating. Just threw it out there without thinking. I apologized right away, I mean immediately, but the damage was done. For two years, there was this strain between us. We'd see each other at church and there was this... wall. You could feel it.

    The Point: Sometimes apologies don't immediately heal what's been broken. Words can create lasting damage even when we didn't mean them to.

    Lesson Learned: An immediate apology is good, but some situations require more—they require what Step 9 calls "direct restitution" when the time is right.

    Story 2: The Marriage Decision

    The Story: Right now I'm carrying some heavy stuff. There's a $7,500 legal bill hanging over me. And my wife—she's got serious health conditions. She needs regular hospital treatments for immune system deficiency. It's chronic. Three weeks ago, I actually considered leaving my marriage.

    The Point: Recovery and growth happen within real constraints, not in some fantasy life without problems.

    Lesson Learned: Commitment isn't about staying only when it's easy. It's about deciding to build something beautiful within the actual constraints you have.

    Reflection Questions

    1. Who comes to mind when you consider making direct amends? What feels possible, and what feels daunting about that conversation?
    2. Where might you need to make "living amends"—changing your daily behavior rather than having a single conversation?
    3. Is there someone you've apologized to where the relationship still feels strained? What might "praying for their availability" look like in that situation?
    4. What burdens are you carrying right now that might be making it harder to show up fully in your relationships? How can you acknowledge those burdens without letting them become excuses?

    Suggested Application

    Put these principles into practice with these actionable steps:

  • Create Your List: Write down everyone you've harmed, starting with those where amends feel most urgent.
  • Prayerful Preparation: For each person, spend time in prayer asking for guidance about timing and approach.
  • Categorize: Note which amends can be made directly, which need indirect approaches, and which require professional guidance.
  • Start with the Living Amend: Identify one behavior pattern you can change this week.
  • Schedule First Steps: Choose one person from your list and set a realistic timeframe.
